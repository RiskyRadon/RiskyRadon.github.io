This is radon

Radon is an element in the Periodic Table and part of the noble gas family. It’s a product of the natural radioactive decay of Radium, causing it to be radioactive itself. It’s commonly found as a gas, and sometimes dissolved in water. Its gaseous form can cause lung cancer if inhaled, and according to the EPA, is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the US today.

Dangers of Radon

Radon is the second highest cause of lung cancer in the United States next to smoking. It leads to 21,000 deaths per year due to lung cancer. Radon provides even more of a risk for those who are current or former smokers. The odds of someone getting lung cancer who smokes and lives with high levels of Radon in their home is from 1/7 to 1/3.

How it enters the home

Soil

Radon can enter a house from the soil underneath. Due to pressure differences between the inside of a house (usually lower than in the soil) and the soil, gases like radon can get sucked in through cracks in the foundation.

Water

Radon is commonly dissolved in the groundwater that flows through granite, and can seep into private wells. Radon can be both released into the air and breathed in (though only 1-2% of radon in the air comes from water) or consumed as part of drinking water. Drinking water with high levels of radon can cause stomach cancer.

Construction Material

Though rare, and accounts for very little of the radon in the air, building materials such as sandstone, brick, natural stone, gypsum, and granite all contain trace amounts of radium, uranium, and thorium, which all decay into radon.

Outdoor Air

Radon is commonly found in the outdoor air, but exists in very low concentrations. Radon is primarly found inside the home, and that is where it has the greatest impact on air quality.
