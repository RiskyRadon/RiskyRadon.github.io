This is radon

Radon is an element in the Periodic Table and part of the noble gas family. It’s a product of the natural radioactive decay of Radium, causing it to be radioactive itself. It’s commonly found as a gas, and sometimes dissolved in water. Its gaseous form can cause lung cancer if inhaled, and according to the EPA, is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the US today.

How it enters the home

Soil

Radon can enter a house from the soil underneath. Due to pressure differences between the inside of a house (usually lower than in the soil) and the soil, gases like radon can get sucked in through cracks in the foundation.

Water

Radon is commonly dissolved in the groundwater that flows through granite, and can seep into private wells. Radon in the water supply can be released into the home when the water is exposed to the air through use (e.g. showers, baths, kitchen/bathroom faucets).

Construction Material

Though rare, and accounts for very little of the radon in the air, building materials such as sandstone, brick, natural stone, gypsum, and granite all contain trace amounts of radium, uranium, and thorium, which all decay into radon.

Outdoor Air

Radon is commonly found in the outdoor air, but exists in very low concentrations. Radon is primarly found inside the home, and that is where it has the greatest impact on air quality.

Dangers of Radon

Radon is measured in pCi/L, or picocuries per liter. The EPA recommends fixing the home at >2 pCi/L, but even at the average indoor radon level of 1.3 pCi/L, long-term exposure can cause lung cancer. Additionally, the EPA estimates that 1 in 15 homes have high radon levels, leading to around 21,000 deaths per year due to radon-related lung cancer.

Testing is one of the only ways to know if your house has radon. Continue below to learn more about testing and mitigation

Testing and Mitigation

1. Testing

Find short-term radon testing kits (3-7 days) at city and county health departments or at a local home improvement store. Though they can give a rough estimate of the radon levels in your house in the short term, a long term test is recommended given the fluctuations in seasonal radon levels. The EPA recommends testing for radonevery two years, and especially before buying, selling, or renovating a house.

2. How to test (short term)

Hang the sampler in the center of the "lowest area of the building used on a regular basis" (for most, this is the basement). If it cannot be hanged from the ceiling, place it on a table within the normal breathing zone (2 to 6 feet from the floor). Do not activate any fans, place the sampler in a draft, or place it next to a heat source. Do not test in humid rooms such as bathrooms, kitchens, or laundry rooms. Leave the sampler for at least three days. Follow instructions on the packaging and send the device to the laboratory as directed.

3. Long term testing

Short term testing can be effective to test for high radon levels, but doesn't account for seasonal flucturations. Long term testing is still recommended. Long term testers can be expensive, but give a better idea of average radon levels over time. More Info

4. Getting rid of radon

This is the most common method, which involves installing a pump that sucks soil gass from underneath the house before it can enter the house. After collecting the gas, it is fanned out to the outdoor air and dispersed.
Sealing + link
Cracks, loose pipes, and sump pits can be sealed to not allow gas exchange between the house and the soil. Additionally, good sealing and construction practicess can act as a preventative measure to inhibit radon from entering the home.
